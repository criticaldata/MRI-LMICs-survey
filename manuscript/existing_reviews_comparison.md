# Comparison with Existing Reviews

Several prior reviews have addressed aspects of MRI super-resolution or low-field MRI, but none have combined these perspectives with a systematic assessment of translational potential for low-resource settings.

**Li et al. (2022)** [CITATION] provided a comprehensive technical survey of deep learning methods for MRI super-resolution, cataloguing architectures and benchmark performance. However, their scope was limited to methodological taxonomy and quantitative comparisons on standard high-field datasets, with no consideration of deployment feasibility in resource-constrained environments.

**Chen et al. (2023)** [CITATION] broadened the lens to deep learning super-resolution across multiple medical imaging modalities, including CT, ultrasound, and MRI. While valuable for cross-modality comparison, the review did not distinguish between high-field and low-field MRI contexts and did not assess code availability, computational requirements, or LMIC applicability.

**Iglesias et al. (2023)** [CITATION] reviewed the emerging landscape of low-field MRI, covering hardware developments, clinical applications, and image enhancement strategies. Their treatment of super-resolution was necessarily brief and non-systematic, as it was one component of a broader low-field review rather than the primary focus.

**Our contribution** is the first systematic review that sits at the intersection of these bodies of work. We combine a rigorous survey of deep learning SR methods (56 studies, 2020--2025) with a novel LMIC-relevance scoring framework, quantitative assessment of open science practices (code sharing, data availability), and explicit evaluation of whether current methods address the technical realities of low-field, low-resource MRI deployment. This intersection -- SR methodology evaluated through a translational equity lens -- has not been addressed in any prior review.
