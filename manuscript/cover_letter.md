# Cover Letter

Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, "Deep Learning Super-Resolution for MRI: Technical Advances and Translational Potential for Low-Resource Settings," for consideration as a Review Article in *Nature Machine Intelligence*.

We believe this work is well suited to NMI because it sits at the intersection of machine learning methodology and global health equity -- a combination that speaks directly to the journal's commitment to examining the societal implications of AI technologies.

**Timeliness.** The convergence of two trends makes this review especially timely. First, portable low-field MRI systems (e.g., Hyperfine Swoop, 64 mT) are expanding neuroimaging access in sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, and emergency settings worldwide. Second, deep learning super-resolution methods have matured rapidly, with architectures now capable of substantial image quality enhancement at modest computational cost. Yet no review has systematically assessed whether these SR methods can actually bridge the gap between low-field acquisition and diagnostic-quality imaging in the settings that need it most.

**Unique contribution.** Existing reviews of MRI super-resolution focus exclusively on technical performance metrics among high-resource institutions. Our work is the first to overlay a translational lens: we developed and applied a novel LMIC-relevance scoring framework to 56 studies (2020--2025), evaluating each on dimensions including low-field applicability, computational requirements, code availability, and explicit consideration of resource-constrained deployment.

**Key findings that will interest the ML community.** Our analysis reveals critical gaps. Only 7% of studies directly target LMIC settings, and just 16% share their source code -- a striking shortfall for a field that relies on reproducibility. From a methodological standpoint, we identify open challenges in distribution shift between high-field training data and low-field inference, domain adaptation without paired data, and the design of efficient architectures suitable for edge deployment. These are precisely the kind of ML problems -- robustness, generalization, and efficiency under constraints -- that drive innovation across the field.

**Scope and structure.** The review synthesizes 56 papers across architecture taxonomy (CNNs, GANs, transformers, diffusion models), application domains (brain, cardiac, musculoskeletal), and evaluation practices. We provide a quantitative summary of performance benchmarks (median PSNR 34.1 dB, SSIM 0.913) and conclude with a concrete roadmap for LMIC-oriented research priorities.

We confirm that this manuscript has not been published elsewhere and is not under consideration at another journal. All authors have approved the submitted version.

Thank you for your consideration. We look forward to your response.
